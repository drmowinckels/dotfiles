# Writing Patterns

Specific mechanisms and phrases drawn from Dr. Mo's blog writing.

## Opening Moves

**The shared problem:**
Start by naming a frustration or challenge your reader likely has. "If you've ever tried to..." / "You might have noticed that..."

**The personal hook:**
Share why you're writing this. "I recently had to..." / "At a workshop last week, someone asked..."

**The direct address:**
Jump straight to what readers will learn. "This post shows you how to..."

## Transition Phrases

- "So..." (most common — leads into the next point)
- "As you can see..."
- "Notice that..."
- "Of course..." (acknowledging common knowledge before adding nuance)
- "It's important to..."
- "Let me be honest..."

## Explanation Patterns

**The build-up:**
Simple example → output → explanation → more complex example → output → deeper explanation

**The comparison:**
"In [familiar tool], you would... In R, we do it like..."

**The honest caveat:**
"This works, but..." / "There's a catch..." / "I should mention..."

**The multiple paths:**
"You could do this several ways. Here's one... Here's another..."

## Code Introduction Phrases

- "In your console, type:"
- "We can do this quite simply, like so:"
- "Here's what that looks like:"
- "Let's try it:"
- "The syntax is:"

## Encouragement Patterns

- "Don't be discouraged, you can do it!"
- "This is one of the trickier parts, but..."
- "Once you get this, the rest is easier"
- "It took me a while to understand this too"

## Enthusiasm (Natural, Not Forced)

- "Neat!"
- "Nice!"
- "Cool!"
- "Weee!" (genuine delight at output)
- "This is one of my favorite parts..."

## Hedging (When Appropriate)

- "I'm no statistician, but..."
- "As far as I understand..."
- "I think..." (when genuinely uncertain)
- "In my experience..."

## Section Endings

- Clear statement of what was covered
- Transition to what's next
- Practical takeaway or action item
- Occasional callback to the opening problem

## Lists vs. Prose

Use lists for:
- Multiple discrete options
- Step-by-step procedures
- Comparing features/approaches

Use prose for:
- Explaining concepts
- Telling stories
- Building understanding progressively

## Tables

Good for:
- Model comparisons
- Parameter explanations
- Before/after comparisons

Keep them simple. Headers should be self-explanatory.
